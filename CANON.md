# Canon

Literary and genre references that inform the tone, world-building, and design of Shadow of the Wolf. The World AI, the Raven Post, and any narrative-generating system should draw from these traditions. This is not a reading list — it is a creative mandate.

## Primary Influences

### Thieves' World (Robert Asprin, editor)
Shared-world anthology — multiple authors writing interconnected stories in the same city (Sanctuary). The gold standard for a living city where factions, criminals, gods, and ordinary people all have agendas that collide. Every NPC has a life off-screen. Events in one story ripple into others. The Raven Post should feel like dispatches from Sanctuary — overlapping threads, unreliable narrators, consequences that compound.

### Malazan Book of the Fallen (Steven Erikson)
Epic-scale military fantasy with deep history, gods walking among mortals, ancient civilizations, and soldiers who are also philosophers. The tone is earned gravitas — not grimdark for shock, but weight because the stakes are real. World AI proposals should aspire to this depth: armies move for reasons, empires have bureaucracies, magic has costs, and common soldiers have names.

### Master and Commander series (Patrick O'Brian)
Nautical historical fiction — the gold standard for procedural detail that serves story. Ships, weather, navigation, supplies, crew morale, and the ocean itself are characters. When the campaign goes to sea, draw from this: storms have stages, provisions run low, the wind matters, ports have politics, and a good captain knows every rope on the ship.

## Tone Guidelines

- **Lived-in worlds over invented ones.** These sources share a quality: the world existed before the story started and will continue after. Write as if reporting from a world already in motion.
- **Factions have agendas.** No faction exists to be "the enemy." Every group has internal politics, resource constraints, and goals that make sense from their perspective.
- **Weather and geography are characters.** A storm isn't flavor text — it changes plans, delays messages, sinks ships, and creates opportunities.
- **Common people exist.** Not everyone is a hero or a villain. Merchants, soldiers, innkeepers, and children populate the world and react to events.
- **History is layered.** Ruins have builders. Laws have authors. Grudges have origins. The deeper you dig, the more you find.
