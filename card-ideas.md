# Blackmoor — Card System Ideas

## Card Types

- **Spell Cards** — one-time castings that don't cost spell slots. Non-casters can use lower-level spell cards. Class restrictions on 5th-level+
- **Combat Cards** — direct mechanical effects: +to hit, extra damage, impose disadvantage, force rerolls
- **Influence Cards** — social and exploration effects: persuasion advantage, NPC favors, forged documents, disguises
- **Defense Cards** — damage reduction, auto-succeed saves, temp HP, redirect attacks, spell deflection
- **Wild Cards** — unpredictable effects, roll-on-a-table chaos, body swaps, gravity reversal, mimics
- **Blessing Cards** — persistent buffs lasting one encounter or one session (+1 to hit, +2 saves, speed boost, maximized healing)
- **Curse Cards** — persistent debuffs that are hard to get rid of (inverted risk = low number means the curse sticks)
- **Trophy Cards** — prestige items with powerful one-shot effects (resurrection, reforging, legendary summons)

## Risk of Loss (d20)

- Every card play triggers a d20 roll
- Roll at or below the card's risk number = card is lost
- Higher risk number = more likely to lose the card
- The stronger the card, the higher the risk — power is always temporary
- Common cards: risk 2-4 (10-20% loss chance) — reliable workhorses
- Uncommon cards: risk 5-8 (25-40%) — real gamble each use
- Rare cards: risk 9-13 (45-65%) — coin flip territory
- Legendary cards: risk 14-18 (70-90%) — almost certainly gone after one use
- Cursed cards: risk 2-4 (inverted — low risk means the curse is hard to shed)
- Nat 1 on risk roll could have extra consequences (card goes to opponent, becomes cursed, etc.)

## Charge System

- Cards returned to the marketplace come back "empty" at 0% charge
- Cards recharge over real time (Common: 24h, Uncommon: 48-72h, Rare: 1 week)
- Partially charged cards are usable at reduced effect and sold at a discount
- Fully charged cards command premium prices
- Even successful plays drain charge (25-50% per use) — cards degrade over time regardless of risk rolls
- This prevents lucky rollers from keeping a powerful card forever

## Fast Recharge

- Casters can spend magic points to instantly recharge magic cards (costs scale with card power)
- Trade-off: a caster who fast-recharges arrives at the session with fewer spell resources
- Martial classes can spend gold or downtime actions to accelerate martial card recharge
- Each class has a path to card readiness, just using different currencies

## Consumed vs Rechargeable

- **Consumed (destroyed on use, regardless of risk roll):**
  - Healing spells and potions
  - Poisons
  - Scrolls
  - One-shot trophies (Phoenix Tear, Soul Ember, etc.)
  - Forged papers
  - Food/drink buffs
- **Rechargeable (returns to marketplace empty when lost):**
  - Weapon enchantment cards
  - Blessings and buffs
  - Combat technique cards
  - Influence cards
  - Defense cards
  - Wild cards

## Where Lost Cards Go

- **Marketplace (primary destination)** — rechargeable cards return at 0% charge, recharge over real time
- **Destroyed** — consumed cards and legendaries, gone forever
- Cards returned to the marketplace create a living economy: supply fluctuates, prices respond

## Symbol Combinations

- Each card has one or more symbols printed on it (e.g., flame, shield, skull, eye, leaf, star, moon, lightning)
- Two cards sharing a matching symbol can be played together in the same turn for a combo effect
- Combo effects are stronger than playing either card alone (bonus damage, extended duration, wider area, etc.)
- Players must discover which symbols pair well — not all combos are equal
- Encourages collecting cards with complementary symbols rather than just hoarding the most powerful individual cards
- Adds a deck-building strategy layer: curating your hand for combo potential, not just raw power
- Trading becomes more nuanced — a card might be average on its own but essential for completing a powerful combo
- Rare or legendary cards could have unique symbols that only match one or two other cards in the entire set
- DM can introduce new symbols tied to campaign arcs (a "void" symbol appears on cards related to the current villain)

## Egalitarian Balance

- Hand limit (5-7 cards per session) — everyone has the same number of slots, the question is which cards fill them
- Risk of loss prevents hoarding — the more you play, the more you lose
- Powerful cards are self-balancing — highest power = highest risk = shortest lifespan
- Marketplace equalizes access — players who can't forage much can buy cards with in-game gold
- Trading creates social equity — class-gated drops encourage swaps between players
- Combo symbols reward smart curation over raw accumulation

## Card Trading

- Player-to-player trades at the table (mid-session negotiation)
- Online trade offers between sessions (propose swaps, counter-offers)
- Trade cards exist that are only useful to other classes — natural incentive to swap
- Physical card exchange at the table creates social moments

## DM's Hand (Enemy Cards)

- DM accumulates cards from player losses during sessions
- Boss encounters can be armed with the party's own lost cards
- Defeating an enemy who played a card can yield that card as loot
- Dramatic reveal: show the party a physical card they recognize being played against them
- The better the cards players have been using, the more dangerous the next boss becomes

## Crafting Cards (Workshops)

- Spell Workshop: combine arcane materials into spell cards
- Potions Workshop: brew consumable potion cards from organic/elemental materials
- Forging Workshop: create weapon/armor enhancement cards from mineral materials
- Magic Item Workshop: enchant gear cards using arcane + mineral materials, socket system for upgrades
- Recipes discovered through lore, experimentation, or purchased from NPCs
- Crafting checks (d20 + proficiency) determine quality: high roll = bonus properties, nat 1 = cursed, nat 20 = masterwork
- Crafting takes real time (hours to days depending on power level)

## Physical Cards at the Table

- Printed cards with art, stats, flavor text for tactile play
- Physical sacrifice mechanic — tear up or burn a card for a powerful in-game crafting effect
- Trophy cards brought from the field grant bonuses when presented at the session
- Cursed cards physically handed back to the DM or traded to another player
- The evidence board: players lay out cards, lore fragments, and letters on the table — connections emerge over time
